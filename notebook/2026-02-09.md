# 2026-02-09

## Objective
Finalize the hardware strategy for the MPPT subsystem.

## Record of what was done
Evaluated options for measuring panel power. Initially researched all-in-one MPPT chips (like the TI BQ24650) to handle tracking and battery charging simultaneously.

### Pros of All-in-One ICs
- Simplifies circuit design.
- Handles battery charging automatically.
- Reduces software load on the MCU.

### Cons of All-in-One ICs
- Acts as a black box, making it difficult to extract exact voltage and current telemetry.
- Requires a battery to operate.

### Pros of Discrete ICs
- Gives the ESP32 direct access to raw panel data, allowing us to run the Perturb and Observe algorithm ourselves.
- Lets us feed exact telemetry into our dirt detection logic.

### Cons of Discrete ICs
- Requires more PCB routing.
- Consumes MCU processing power to generate the 50 kHz PWM.

## Decision
Opted to use a discrete voltage divider and a dedicated current sense IC to measure power, feeding those analog values into the ESP32 to maintain full control over the diagnostic data.

## System Design Note
Realized a critical flaw in testing: the robot is powered via grid AC, meaning the solar panel output is currently completely isolated. If the panel is open-circuited, no current flows, and we cannot measure power.

## Next Steps
We need to design a load for the solar panels to dump power into (potentially a heavy dummy load resistor) so the MPPT algorithm has actual current to track.
